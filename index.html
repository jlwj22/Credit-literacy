üìñ What is Credit?

1 2 3 4 5

Let's start with the basics

üí° Credit Definition

Credit is the ability to borrow money or access goods and services with the understanding that you'll pay for them later.

Think of credit like a financial trust system. When someone gives you credit, they're trusting that you'll pay them back according to your agreement.

üåü Real-Life Example

Imagine you want to buy a $500 laptop but only have $200 in your bank account. With a credit card, you can purchase the laptop today and pay the credit card company back over time.

How Does Credit Work?

Credit involves three key players:

üè¶
Lender

Banks, credit card companies, or other institutions that provide credit

üë§
Borrower (You)

The person who receives credit and promises to pay it back

üìä
Credit Bureaus

Companies that track your credit history and create your credit report

The Process: When you apply for credit, lenders check your credit history to decide if they'll lend to you and what terms they'll offer.

Types of Credit

There are several different types of credit you might encounter:

üí≥ Revolving Credit

Examples: Credit cards, lines of credit

You can borrow up to a limit, pay it back, and borrow again.

üè† Installment Credit

Examples: Car loans, mortgages, student loans

You borrow a fixed amount and pay it back in regular payments.

üîÑ Open Credit

Examples: Utility bills, charge cards

You must pay the full balance each month.

Why Does Credit Matter?

Having good credit opens doors to many financial opportunities:

üè†
Buy a Home

Most people need a mortgage to buy a house

üöó
Finance a Car

Get better interest rates on auto loans

üí∞
Lower Interest Rates

Save money on all types of loans

üì±
Phone Plans

Get cell phone contracts without deposits

üè¢
Rent Apartments

Landlords often check credit scores

üíº
Job Opportunities

Some employers check credit for certain positions

  • Building credit early sets you up for future success

What is a FICO Score?

üí° FICO Score Definition

A FICO Score is a three-digit number (300-850) that represents your creditworthiness. It tells lenders how likely you are to repay borrowed money on time.

üè¢ Created by Fair Isaac Corporation

FICO stands for Fair Isaac Corporation, the company that created this scoring model in 1989. It's now the most widely used credit scoring system.

üåü Why It Matters

Your FICO score affects:

  • Whether you get approved for loans and credit cards
  • The interest rates you'll pay
  • Credit limits you'll receive
  • Sometimes even job opportunities and housing

üí≥ Credit vs Debit Cards

1 2 3 4

How Credit and Debit Cards Work

üí° The Basic Difference

Both cards look similar and work at most places, but they handle your money very differently.

üè¶
Debit Cards

Your Own Money

How it works:
  • Connected to your checking account
  • Uses money you already have
  • Transactions happen immediately
  • When you spend $50, $50 leaves your account right away
Example Transaction:
1. You have $500 in your account
2. You buy groceries for $100
3. Now you have $400 in your account
üí≥
Credit Cards

Borrowed Money

How it works:
  • Connected to a line of credit
  • Uses money lent by the bank
  • You pay the bank back later
  • When you spend $50, you owe the bank $50
Example Transaction:
1. You have $1000 credit limit
2. You buy groceries for $100
3. You owe the bank $100, $900 credit left
üéØ Key Insight

Debit cards spend your money. Credit cards create debt that you must pay back, usually with interest if you don't pay in full.

Advantages and Disadvantages

üè¶ Debit Cards
‚úÖ Advantages
  • Can't overspend (limited by account balance)
  • No interest charges
  • No monthly bills to track
  • Easy to get (just need a bank account)
  • Helps control spending naturally
  • ATM access for cash
‚ùå Disadvantages
  • Doesn't build credit history
  • Less fraud protection
  • Overdraft fees if you overspend
  • Limited dispute resolution
  • No purchase rewards/cash back
  • Hard to rent cars or hotels
üí≥ Credit Cards
‚úÖ Advantages
  • Builds credit history and score
  • Strong fraud protection
  • Purchase rewards and cash back
  • Extended warranties on purchases
  • Emergency money when needed
  • Better for car rentals and hotels
  • Grace period for payments
‚ùå Disadvantages
  • Easy to overspend and get into debt
  • High interest rates (15-25%+ APR)
  • Monthly bills to manage
  • Can hurt credit if misused
  • Annual fees on some cards
  • Temptation to spend money you don't have
üìã When to Use Each Card
üëç Better for Debit Cards:
  • ATM cash withdrawals
  • When you're learning to budget
  • Small everyday purchases
  • When you want to avoid debt
üëç Better for Credit Cards:
  • Online purchases
  • Large purchases (with good plan to pay off)
  • Travel expenses
  • Building credit history
  • Emergency situations

How to Use Credit Cards Responsibly

üèÜ The Golden Rules of Credit Cards
1
Only Spend What You Can Afford

Before using your credit card, ask: "Do I have this money in my bank account to pay this off?"

2
Pay Your Full Balance Every Month

This avoids interest charges and builds excellent credit history.

3
Keep Utilization Low

Use less than 30% of your credit limit, ideally under 10%.

4
Always Pay At Least the Minimum

Late payments hurt your credit score more than anything else.

‚ö†Ô∏è Common Credit Card Mistakes to Avoid
üí∏
Making Only Minimum Payments

A $1,000 balance at 18% APR takes 5+ years to pay off with minimums only!

üõçÔ∏è
Using Credit for Lifestyle Inflation

Don't use credit to live beyond your means or buy things you can't afford.

üí≥
Maxing Out Credit Cards

High utilization hurts your credit score and makes it hard to pay off balances.

üìÖ
Missing Payment Due Dates

Late payments can cost $25-40 in fees plus damage to your credit score.

üß† Smart Credit Card Strategies
ü§ñ
Set Up Automatic Payments

Automate at least the minimum payment to avoid late fees and credit damage.

üì±
Track Spending with Apps

Use your bank's app or budgeting apps to monitor credit card spending in real time.

üí∞
Treat Credit Like Debit

Only use your credit card if you have the money in your checking account to pay for it.

üéÅ
Maximize Rewards Responsibly

Earn cash back and points, but never spend more just to get rewards.

  • Debit cards use your own money; credit cards use borrowed money

  • Shopping (clothes, gadgets)

Maintaining Your Emergency Fund

üìã Emergency Fund Rules
1
Only Use for True Emergencies

Ask yourself: "Is this unexpected, necessary, and urgent?" If all three are yes, it's an emergency.

2
Replenish Immediately

If you use your emergency fund, make it a priority to build it back up as soon as possible.

3
Keep It Separate

Don't mix emergency funds with regular savings or checking accounts.

4
Review Regularly

As your expenses change, adjust your emergency fund target accordingly.
